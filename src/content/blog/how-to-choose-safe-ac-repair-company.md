<h2>100% of new systems must be A2L-compatible: the non-negotiable rule</h2>
<p><a href="https://serviceagent.ai/blogs/hvac-safety-tips/#top" target="_blank" rel="noopener">According to EPA AIM Act via ServiceAgent.ai</a>, all newly installed residential and commercial HVAC systems must transition to next-generation A2L refrigerants, making compliance mandatory across the industry.</p>
<p>If a contractor installs a new central air system, heat pump, or ductless mini-split after January 1, 2025, it must use an A2L refrigerant like R-454B or R-32. The "100%" is absolute.</p>
<p>For you, this means any quote for a new system should explicitly state the refrigerant type. If a technician tries to sell you a new unit with old R-410A, they are either misinformed or attempting to clear illegal inventory.</p>
<p>More critically, their entire team must be trained to handle these new gases safely. Hiring a company not versed in A2Ls for a new installation isn't just unwise, it's potentially dangerous and non-compliant.</p>

<h2>2,088 vs. 466 GWP: why the new refrigerants are here to stay</h2>
<p><a href="https://xoi.io/blog/a2l-refrigerant-transition-2026" target="_blank" rel="noopener">According to refrigerant transition data</a>, R-410A has a Global Warming Potential (GWP) of 2,088, while R-454B reduces that figure to about 466. This 77% reduction is the entire point of the phase-out. But that lower number comes with a trade-off the industry calls "mildly flammable."</p>
<p>This doesn't mean your AC is a bomb. It means that during servicing, if refrigerant leaks near an ignition source, like an electrical spark from a standard tool, it could ignite.</p>
<p>The old ways of doing repairs are now unsafe:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Using any metal tool</li>
  <li>Brazing lines without special purging</li>
</ul>
<p>The new GWP number represents progress for the planet, but it demands progress in technician training and tools. A qualified company won't just have the new refrigerant; they'll have:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Spark-proof recovery machines</li>
  <li>Leak detection tools rated for flammable gases</li>
  <li>Certified training to use them</li>
</ul>

<h2>100% of A2L handling requires specific safety tools and training</h2>
<p><a href="https://serviceagent.ai/blogs/hvac-safety-tips/#top" target="_blank" rel="noopener">According to EPA AIM Act via ServiceAgent.ai</a>, technicians servicing these next-generation refrigerants must use specialized equipment, updated procedures, and manufacturer-approved training protocols.</p>
<p>The "100%" here is about eliminating risk. You cannot safely recover, recharge, or leak-test an A2L system with the tools used for the last 20 years on R-410A. Standard recovery machines have internal electrical contacts that can spark. Standard torches can ignite a leak. The new protocols require:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Spark-proof tools:</strong> Every piece of equipment that touches the refrigerant circuit must be rated for flammable gases.</li>
  <li><strong>Purge procedures:</strong> Before brazing, the lines must be purged with nitrogen to prevent combustion inside the copper.</li>
  <li><strong>Leak detection:</strong> Electronic leak detectors must be specifically calibrated for A2Ls.</li>
  <li><strong>Ventilation:</strong> Technicians must monitor and ensure workspace ventilation during service.</li>
</ul>
<p>An untrained technician with old tools is a legitimate safety hazard in your home. Asking "Are your tools and training updated for A2L refrigerants?" is not a technicality, it's a safety screening question.</p>
